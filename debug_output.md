Tumours of the uveal tract
Introduction
The uveal tract occupies the middle coat of the eye and consists of the iris, ciliary body, and choroid.
The iris has five layers, from anterior to posterior: the anterior border layer, the stroma, the
muscular layer (the dilator and sphincter muscles), the anterior pigment epithelium, and the
posterior pigment epithelium. The ciliary body comprises (from outermost to innermost within the
eye) the supraciliary space, the muscular layer (longitudinal, radial, and circular fibres), the stroma,
the pigmented epithelium, and the non-pigmented epithelium. The choroid comprises (from
outermost to innermost within the eye) the lamina fusca, Haller’s layer, Sattler’s layer, and the
choriocapillaris. These tissues contain melanocytes, fibrocytes, smooth muscle, vascular channels,
and nerves; they can be infiltrated by lymphocytes, histiocytes, and other cells of haematopoietic
origin. The tumours that arise from these tissues include naevi and melanomas (from melanocytes),
schwannomas and neurofibromas (from nerves and nerve sheaths), adenomas and
adenocarcinomas (from the pigmented and non-pigmented epithelia-discussed in Chapter 3), and
lymphoma (from lymphocytes).
For gross and microscopic examination of tumours of the uveal tract, it is advisable to section the
eye such that the centre of the tumour is in the same plane as the centre of the pupil and the centre
of the optic nerve (resulting in a specimen called a P-O section). This can be accomplished by
transilluminating the eye, outlining the transillumination blockage corresponding to the uveal
tumour, and sectioning the eye accordingly. First, a calotte (a small cap) is removed from one side.
The eye is then examined under a dissecting microscope and its gross features described. A
second calotte is then removed from the opposite side of the eye to create a P-O section, from
which slides are then prepared and examined, and the tumour is described in terms of its size,
shape, location, cellular characteristics, and effects on surrounding tissue. Along with the uveal
tract tumour itself, the surrounding tissue and the eye as a whole are also examined
microscopically.
The primary uveal tract malignancy most commonly examined in the pathology laboratory is
melanoma; metastases to the uveal tract are more common clinically, but they are less frequently
examined microscopically. Similarly, naevi are far more common than melanomas, but they are
much less frequently sampled by fine-needle aspiration biopsy. The advent of molecular diagnostic
techniques, such as gene expression profiling and FISH analysis for chromosome 3 aneuploidy,
has enabled small amounts of tumour to be sampled and genetically examined. In general, these
molecular techniques improve the description of a tumour, but they do not replace careful
histological and/or cytological examination.
Clinicopathological correlation is essential for the investigation of uveal tract tumours. For example,
a cystic lesion on the posterior surface of the iris seen on ultrasound biomicroscopy is probably a
cyst of the iris pigment epithelium; when pigmented epithelial cysts of the iris are excised, they
often collapse, revealing essentially normal, redundant iris pigment epithelium. That appearance in
combination with the ultrasound biomicroscopy findings leads to the correct diagnosis. Another
example is a ciliary body tumour that does not block light on transillumination, presenting in a
young woman; these characteristics are clinically suggestive of a leiomyoma (or mesectodermal
leiomyoma), and this should be taken into consideration when evaluating fine-needle aspiration
biopsy or excision specimens from the lesion. Adenomas of the ciliary epithelium appear clinically
as multilobulated lesions, whereas melanomas generally have a smooth surface. Examining
pathologists should be familiar with the clinical characteristics of the uveal tract tumour being
investigated, and they should take these characteristics into consideration when examining the
tumour microscopically.
Within this volume, some of the rarer entities that can arise throughout the uveal tract are
discussed only in their most common site; for example, bilateral diffuse uveal melanocytic
hyperplasia is grouped with tumours of the choroid, and ocular melanocytosis is grouped with
tumours of the iris.

Tumours of the iris
Ocular melanocytosis
Definition:-
Ocular melanocytosis is a congenitally increased population of non-proliferating hyperpigmented
melanocytes.
Related terminology:-
Ocular melanocytosis: melanosis oculi
When occurring together with adjacent cutaneous melanocytosis: oculodermal melanocytosis;
naevus of Ota; naevus fusco-caeruleus ophthalmo-maxillaris
When occurring together with naevus flammeus: phakomatosis pigmentovascularis
Localization:-
Ocular melanocytosis is usually unilateral. It can involve, in a diffuse or sectoral pattern, any or all
of the following: the sclera, iris, ciliary body, choroid, and orbit. Ocular melanocytosis associated
with adjacent cutaneous pigmentation is called oculodermal melanocytosis.
Clinical features:-
Patients present with hyperchromic heterochromia that gives the iris a velvety or mammillated
appearance, which is especially striking if the fellow (uninvolved) eye is blue. There is slate-grey,
patchy pigmentation of the sclera, as well as dark-brown choroid. In oculodermal melanocytosis,
there is cutaneous hyperpigmentation in the trigeminal distribution (V1 or V2); adjacent orbital
tissues may also be involved.
Epidemiology:-
Ocular and oculodermal melanocytoses combined occur in approximately 0.04% of White people,
in 1.4–2.7% of all patients with uveal melanoma, and in 3.1–4.3% of young patients with uveal
melanoma { 26854035 ; 22020174 ; 7133640 ; 24227986 }. These disorders are believed to be
more common among Asians, but prevalence data are not available.
Etiology:-
This is a congenital hamartoma-like disorder.
Pathogenesis:-
The histogenesis of ocular melanocytosis is similar to that of cellular blue naevus and Mongolian
spot; it involves abnormal migration of neural crest–derived melanocytes to the eye and dermis
rather than to the dermoepidermal junction { 13130904 }.
Genetic profile
Somatic activating mutations of GNA11 or GNAQ have been found in some cases associated with
cutaneous melanocytosis, as well as in phakomatosis pigmentovascularis { 26778290 }.
Histopathology:-
The iris and choroid are heavily pigmented and slightly thickened because of increased numbers of
heavily pigmented melanocytes; there is some resemblance to melanocytoma { 6019552 }. Similar
melanocytes are abundant in the ciliary body and between scleral lamellae. Mitoses are not seen.
Thickened areas should be carefully examined to exclude an embedded early uveal melanoma.
Differential diagnosis
The differential diagnosis includes diffuse uveal melanoma and conjunctival melanosis.
Staging (TNM):-
Not applicable.
Prognosis and prediction:-
Ocular melanocytosis in itself is innocuous; however, it is associated with an increased risk of
glaucoma { 27083007 }. It is also an important predisposing factor for uveal melanoma, associated
with a lifetime risk of 1 in 400 { 9442799 }. In atypical cases of uveal melanoma, such as multifocal,
bilateral, and paediatric cases, as well as in orbital and leptomeningeal melanomas, underlying
melanocytosis should be suspected. Melanocytosis-associated uveal melanomas may have a
higher risk of metastasis { 26854035 ; 23522968 ; 23681424 }. The association with uveal
melanoma may be due to a shared predisposing mutation in GNAQ { 19078957 }.

Lisch nodule
Definition:-
A Lisch nodule is an iris hamartoma consisting of a proliferation of melanocytes (a naevus)
occurring in the setting of neurofibromatosis type 1 (NF1). Lisch nodules are one of the seven
National Institutes of Health (NIH) criteria for the diagnosis of NF1 { 3128965 }, and they are the
most common ocular manifestation of the disorder.
ICD-O coding:-
None
Related terminology:-
Sakurai–Lisch nodule; iris hamartoma; melanocytic hamartoma
The term “benign tumour of the iris” has been proposed because of the possible role of ultraviolet
(UV) radiation (presumably via DNA damage) in Lisch nodules’ development { 19516012 }.
Localization:-
Lisch nodules occur in the iris, usually in the inferior hemifield { 19516012 }.
Clinical features:-
Lisch nodules appear in early childhood, first arising in children aged about 2 years. The lesions
present as dome-shaped or confluent nodules, sometimes with ragged borders. They may be
creamy white in dark-coloured irides or brown in light-coloured irides { 27493794 }.
Epidemiology:-
NF1 is an autosomal dominant disorder (phakomatosis); it is the most common of the three types of
neurofibromatosis (the other types being neurofibromatosis type 2 and schwannomatosis). NF1
has an estimated birth prevalence of 1 case per 3000 live births, with no predilection for race, sex,
ethnicity, or geographical location { 10469430 }. Lisch nodules have been found in 95% of all
patients with NF1 and in 100% of those aged ≥ 16 years { 3103673 }.
Etiology:-
The etiological factors include NF1 mutation with other genetic factors, iris colour, and possibly UV
radiation exposure modifiers { 19516012 }.
Pathogenesis:-
The histogenesis is related to NF1 mutation and possibly UV radiation–induced DNA damage
{ 19516012 }.
Genetic profile
NF1 is caused by heterozygous loss of function mutations in the tumour suppressor gene NF1
(chromosome 17q11.2) { 3107130 }, which encodes neurofibromin (NF1), a negative regulator of
RAS signalling. Disease penetrance is 100%, but the phenotypic expressivity varies. De novo
(spontaneous) mutations occur in approximately 50% of affected individuals { 20027112 }.
Genetic susceptibility
Genetic susceptibility is associated with NF1 gene mutations, which show variable expressivity,
even within families; this variability is attributed to genetic and possibly environmental modifiers
{ 19516012 ; 8328449 ; 12214308 }.
Histopathology:-
There is a spindle-type naevus on the anterior surface of the iris.
Differential diagnosis
The differential diagnosis includes naevi, primary melanoma, and (in particular in the absence of
NF1) metastases.
Prognosis and prediction:-
Lisch nodules do not interfere with vision. Patients with NF1 have other ocular abnormalities, such
as plexiform neurofibromas, optic pathway gliomas, and choroidal naevi, as well as cutaneous
neurofibromas. There is an 8–16% lifetime risk of developing malignant peripheral nerve sheath
tumour, generally from a pre-existing plexiform neurofibroma { 12011145 ; 25354145 }. A reported
50–80% of children with NF1 have neurocognitive deficits { 25354145 }.

Melanocytic naevus
Definition:-
Melanocytic naevus of the iris is a benign proliferation of melanocytes, usually pigmented.
ICD-O coding:-
Iris naevus 8720/0
Related terminology:-
Naevus; melanocytoma (which is a specific type of naevus)
Localization:-
Most are located in the inferior half of the iris { 25359901 ; 1305027 }.
Clinical features:-
The majority (> 90%) of iris naevi are pigmented, but some are amelanotic { 6888862 }. They alter
the iris stroma (unlike iris freckles), and there may be an associated cyst. The lesions can be flat or
elevated, and they can be circumscribed or diffuse. Iris naevi have been classified as sectoral,
incomplete sectoral, or solitary { 25359901 }.
Epidemiology:-
Iris naevi, which are more common in light-coloured irides than in dark-coloured irides, occur in
approximately 4% of the population overall. This incidence rate is much lower than that of iris
freckles (which occur in 60% of the population) or choroidal naevi (in 17%) { 14693769 ;
25359901 }. Iris naevi account for 60% of solid melanocytic iris tumours { 22035581 }.
Etiology:-
Sunlight exposure may play an etiological role similar to its role in the formation of iris freckles
{ 28715582 }. The etiology of melanocytic naevi may be multifactorial. Their development may be
related to the migration and maturation of melanoblasts during embryogenesis { 27002320 ;
25359901 ; 19067969 }. Choroidal naevi have been found to be associated with body mass index
and other systemic factors { 26255109 }.
Pathogenesis:-
Genetic profile
GNA11 or GNAQ mutations, found in 83% of uveal melanomas, are also present in a smaller
proportion of cutaneous blue naevi and naevi of Ota, but not in common naevi { 21083380 }.
Recent genetic studies of iris naevi have identified mutations in EIF1AX, NRAS, PTEN, KIT, and
TP53 as well as GNAQ and GNA11 { 28700778 ; 29371009 }. Some iris naevi harbour BAP1
mutations; these cases have been designated iris melanocytic tumours of uncertain malignant
potential { 29371009 }.
Histopathology:-
The naevus cells are bland, usually pigmented, spindle cells with small fusiform nuclei, dispersed
chromatin, and indistinct nucleoli. The nuclei may show a longitudinal fold (chromatin stripe) or
pseudonuclear inclusions. The naevus cells may have elongated cellular processes (dendritic cells),
a more epithelioid appearance, or vacuolated cytoplasm (balloon cells). One classification system
categorizes iris naevi as melanocytomas, spindle cell naevi with surface plaque, spindle cell naevi
with a stromal component, and epithelioid cell naevi { 7305709 }.
Differential diagnosis
The differential diagnosis includes melanoma, metastasis, cysts, and non-melanocytic lesions (e.g.
xanthogranuloma, neurofibroma, and leiomyoma) { 22035581 }.
Staging (TNM):-
Not applicable.
Prognosis and prediction:-
Most naevi are stable. Periodic examinations with photographic documentation and ultrasound are
recommended to monitor for increasing lesion size and other features suggestive of progression to
melanoma { 28141766 }.

Iris melanoma
Definition:-
Iris melanoma is a tumour that arises from the melanocytes of the iris stroma.
ICD-O coding:-
Uveal melanoma 8720/3
Iris melanoma 8720/3
Related terminology:-
Not recommended: Uveal melanoma (less specific, because this includes ciliary body and
choroidal tumours); ocular melanoma (imprecise, discouraged)
Localization:-
Iris melanomas can occur anywhere on the iris, but most (an estimated 80% of cases) are located
in the inferior half of the iris (most commonly at the inferior temporal quadrant) { 23290981 }. Iris
melanomas may invade into the ciliary body, and they may seed the anterior chamber, trabecular
meshwork, and associated aqueous outflow channels.
Clinical features:-
Iris melanomas can have heterogeneous pigmentation, with the tumours ranging from almost
translucent and amelanotic to deeply pigmented. They are usually associated with deformation and
irregularity of the pupillary margin, as well as ectropion uveae, although these characteristics are
not pathognomonic of malignancy. Their margins can be distinct or poorly defined.
Iris melanomas can be nodular and well circumscribed; multinodular; or diffuse with infiltration of
tumour cells and nests over the entire iris surface, lens surface, and trabecular meshwork. They
can also infiltrate into the ciliary body, either in a circumscribed fashion or over the entire
circumference of the trabecular meshwork and ciliary body, producing a so-called ring melanoma.
Ring melanomas are associated with elevated intraocular pressure (secondary glaucoma) that
does not respond to conventional glaucoma medication. The vascularity of the lesions can be
visualized with fluorescein angiography of the anterior segment of the eye (Figs #10454, #10455).
Non-pigmented iris melanomas with multiple tumour nodules over the entire iris surface have been
called tapioca melanomas (Fig #9782). When iris melanomas infiltrate posteriorly, they can touch
the lens, producing a sectorial cataract and/or astigmatism resulting in vision deterioration, which in
some cases might be the first symptom a patient notices (Fig #9783, #9784).
Diffuse iris melanoma may result in heterochromia and secondary glaucoma. Patients with these
symptoms may present late as a result of diagnostic delay and confusion with iridocorneal
endothelial syndrome and/or pigmentary glaucoma.
Epidemiology:-
Iris melanomas account for 5% of all uveal melanomas.
Etiology:-
A possible association with ultraviolet (UV) radiation has been proposed, because most iris
melanomas are located in the inferior half of the iris (most commonly at the inferior temporal
quadrant), where UV irradiation due to sun exposure is most prominent { 23290981 }.
Pathogenesis:-
Iris melanomas arise from neural crest– derived melanocytes, which are normally scattered within
the iris stroma. There is a reported tendency for cytological dedifferentiation from normal
melanocytes to the spindle cell variant, and further to the epithelioid phenotype, during the natural
evolution of these lesions { 1794785 }.
Genetic profile
Iris melanomas show chromosomal aberrations similar to those seen in choroidal melanomas.
Cytogenetically, most iris melanomas have a low-metastatic-risk profile, reflecting their relatively
benign clinical course { 27098748 ; 21336070 }. The mutations found in iris melanoma, which are
similar to those found in choroidal and ciliary body melanomas, include mutations of GNAQ,
GNA11, EIF1AX, and SF3B1 { 28700778 ; 29371009 }, as well as BAP1 { 25974357 ; 29371009 }.
In one study, iris melanomas were found to show mutations similar to those seen in cutaneous
melanoma, namely, mutations in NRAS, BRAF, PTEN, KIT, and TP53 { 29371009 }. However,
these findings contrast with those of other studies, in which no BRAF mutations were detected in
any of the examined iris melanoma cases { 27098748 }.
Genetic susceptibility
The familial BAP1 tumour predisposition syndrome is associated with uveal melanomas.
Macroscopic appearance:-
See Fig #9783.
Histopathology:-
Iris melanomas can have very heterogeneous pigmentation, varying from completely amelanotic to
densely pigmented. They may have blander morphology than do choroidal and ciliary body
melanomas (Figs #9794, #9786, #9785, #9878).
Cytopathologically, iris melanomas can display the full spectrum of melanocytic differentiation, from
spindle cells to epithelioid cells. Spindle cell nuclei can have fine linear interdigitation of their
nuclear membrane, usually referred to as a chromatin strip, with small nucleoli (spindle B cells) or
without small nucleoli (spindle A cells). At the other end of the differentiation spectrum, iris
melanomas can be composed of epithelioid cells, which are usually less pigmented than spindle
cells (or even amelanotic). Epithelioid cells may be seen at the base (ciliary body side) of iris
melanomas. The parts of iris melanomas located on the surface of the iris and protruding towards
the anterior chamber are cytologically more loosely and non-coherently arranged, which increases
the risk of dispersion of cells within the anterior chamber upon surgical manipulation. Iris
melanomas that are biopsied for diagnostic purposes usually have a smaller spindle cell,
well-differentiated melanocytic morphology, with small or even absent nucleoli, probably because
they are captured (clinically and histopathologically) at an early stage of their dedifferentiation
process (Figs #9841, #9842).
On immunohistochemistry, iris melanomas exhibit the same features as choroidal and ciliary body
melanomas, namely, positivity for S100 protein, HMB45 antigen, and melan-A. In one study, 189
lesions originally diagnosed as iris melanomas were retrospectively reclassified; 87% were
reassigned to one of six categories of naevi and 13% were classified as melanomas { 7305709 }.
Melanoma cells on the iris surface can undergo aqueous humour modification and appear smaller
(with a naevoid appearance) than the larger melanoma cells in the stroma beneath { 19019926 }.
These modified surface cells have a lower proliferation rate and express pro-differentiation proteins
{ 19019926 }.
Differential diagnosis
The differential diagnosis includes naevi and melanocytomas, metastatic lesions to the iris,
leiomyoma of the iris stroma, non-neoplastic granulomatous lesions, and infectious infiltrates.
Cytology:-
See Fig #9813.
Staging (TNM):-
Iris melanomas are staged according to the UICC 8th edition TNM classification for iris melanomas
(separate from ciliary body and choroidal melanomas).
Prognosis and prediction:-
The prognosis of iris melanoma is good, because most lesions are treated at an early stage.
According to a Kaplan–Meier life table analysis, metastasis occurs in 3% of patients by 5 years
after diagnosis, 5% by 10 years, and 10% by 20 years. Clinical factors at the time of initial
evaluation that are prognostic of eventual metastasis from iris melanoma include older patient age,
elevated intraocular pressure, posterior tumour margin at the angle or iris root (vs the midzone),
extraocular extension, and prior surgical treatment of the tumour before referral (vs observation).
The method of management (i.e. resection, radiation therapy, or enucleation) does not affect the
risk of metastasis { 11150284 }.

Implantation cyst
Definition:-
Implantation cyst of the iris is a squamous epithelium–lined cyst.
ICD-O coding:-
None
Related terminology:-
Primary iris stromal cyst; acquired epithelial implantation cyst
Localization:-
Iris stromal cysts can arise anywhere on or in the iris, but they occur most commonly in the
mid-periphery and the inferior or superonasal quadrant { 9663240 }.
Clinical features:-
Iris stromal cysts have a thin, slightly translucent anterior wall, allowing visualization of the iris
pigment epithelium through the (usually atrophic) underlying iris stroma. The cysts contain a clear
or slightly opaque fluid and can be septated by thin translucent membranes. They tend to enlarge
towards the chamber angle and optical axis, inducing secondary glaucoma, corneal oedema, and
amblyopia { 8352310 ; 9663240 ; 20490997 }.
Etiology:-
Iris stromal cysts are defined as primary if there is no recognizable cause and secondary if they are
induced by penetrating anterior chamber injury. Epithelial, mesenchymal, and/or conjunctival goblet
cells can be introduced into the anterior chamber as a result of trauma, giving rise to a secondary
implantation cyst { 8352310 ; 20490997 }.
Pathogenesis:-
Iris stromal cysts probably originate as a result of the implantation of cells from the eye surface into
the anterior chamber, iris surface, or iris stroma; this may occur at various points during prenatal
development or after birth. Some cases have been associated with penetrating trauma, including
inadvertent penetration of the anterior chamber during amniocentesis.
Histopathology:-
Primary iris stromal cysts are lined by multilayered stratified squamous epithelium, with or without
goblet cells. Keratinization has been found in some cases, and corneal endothelial cells covering
the cyst wall have also been described.
Differential diagnosis
The differential diagnosis includes pigmented and non-pigmented tumours of the iris such as
melanoma, melanocytoma, iris metastases, leiomyoma, juvenile xanthogranuloma, and various
types of inflammatory and autoimmune-associated granulomas of the iris surface and stroma
{ 15492747 }.
Prognosis and prediction:-
Surgical excision or drainage may be necessary, because of the high probability of progressive
enlargement of the cyst with involvement of the visual axis and the development of complications
such as secondary glaucoma and cataract { 15305534 }.

Ectopic lacrimal gland
Definition:-
Ectopic lacrimal gland in the iris is a choristoma composed of lacrimal gland tissue.
ICD-O coding:-
None
Related terminology:-
Heterotopic lacrimal gland
Localization:-
Ectopic lacrimal gland in the iris is found in the stroma of the iris.
Clinical features:-
This ectopia is typically diagnosed in infants or children aged < 6 years. It presents as a fleshy pink
mass with an irregular nodular surface and may appear vascularized { 17592332 ; 10844067 }. The
lesion can become cystic and expand, obstructing the iridocorneal angle and leading to secondary
closed-angle glaucoma { 9930171 }. In most reported cases, resection or enucleation of the eye
was necessary for definitive histological diagnosis. In some cases, it may be possible to diagnose
this lesion by fine-needle aspiration biopsy { 17592332 }.
Epidemiology:-
The incidence in unknown. Fewer than 20 case reports of ectopic lacrimal gland in the iris have
been published, with most of the reported cases occurring in an infant or young child { 7832229 ;
10844067 }. Most lacrimal ectopias are found in extraocular sites (such as the orbit and conjunctiva)
{ 5152651 ; 3684203 }.
Etiology:-
Possible etiologies include displacement of lacrimal buds during closure of the fetal fissure,
implantation of surface ectoderm during lens formation, extension of lacrimal tissue before the
closure of scleral defects during fetal life, and aberrant differentiation of pluripotent intraocular cells
{ 3991133 }.
Pathogenesis:-
Lacrimal gland ectopias consist of benign lacrimal tissue in an ectopic site. They may arise during
fetal life or as a result of aberrant differentiation of pluripotent intraocular cells.
Histopathology:-
Ectopic lacrimal tissue has the histological appearance of benign acini and ducts of the normal
lacrimal gland. The ducts can become cystically dilated (dacryops), and their lining epithelium may
become attenuated.
Differential diagnosis
A number of clinical differential diagnoses exist, but ectopic lacrimal gland in the iris is histologically
distinctive and readily diagnosed.
Prognosis and prediction:-
Clinical follow-up is recommended, to prevent vision loss due to lesional growth and secondary
glaucoma { 7832229 }. Phthisis of the eye can result in cases not being treated. Malignant
transformation has not been reported.

Tumours of the ciliary body and choroid
Choroidal and ciliary body naevi
Definition:-
Choroidal and ciliary body naevi are benign neoplasms arising from choroidal and ciliary body
melanocytes.
ICD-O coding:-
Uveal naevus 8720/0
Localization:-
These naevi occur in the pars plicata or pars plana of the ciliary body or choroid, most often in the
post-equatorial choroid outside the foveolar region { 28141766 }.
Clinical features:-
Ciliary body naevi typically go undetected unless they are associated with anterior extension of the
lesion through the iris root, focal shallowing of the anterior chamber, a sectoral cataract, episcleral
sentinel vessels, or extraocular extension { 1704157 ; 26659447 }[[Singh AD (2007). Tumors of the
uvea: benign melanocytic tumors. In: Singh AD, Damato BE, Pe’er J, Murphree AL, Perry J, editors.
Clinical ophthalmic oncology. Philadelphia: Elsevier; pp. 185–97.]] Ultrasound biomicroscopy and
anterior segment optical coherence tomography are useful ancillary examinations { 25091953 ;
23219068 ; 20346778 }. Choroidal naevi typically appear as a flat or slightly elevated patch of
increased pigmentation or a yellowish amelanotic lesion measuring < 5 mm in diameter and < 2
mm in thickness { 18067966 }; they often have irregular or feathered borders.
Epidemiology:-
The prevalence of ciliary body naevi is unknown, because data are available only from autopsy and
archival studies. In 152 consecutive autopsies, a ciliary body naevus was found in 3% of the 200
eyes studied { 5838370 }. In the Armed Forces Institute of Pathology (AFIP) registry, 6% of 102
uveal naevi in 100 enucleated eyes were located in the ciliary body { 5924936 }. A number of
studies have reported the prevalence of choroidal naevi in adults { 21820181 ; 18519073 ;
22872672 ; 19273796 }. The highest prevalence, estimated at 4–7%, is found in White populations
{ 21820181 ; 9596501 }.
Etiology:-
The etiology is unknown, but choroidal and ciliary body naevi are generally assumed to be
congenital lesions that may grow slowly during childhood and early adulthood { 20801518 }.
Pathogenesis:-
The naevus cells are derived from melanocytes of neural crest origin.
Genetic profile
In one study, mutually exclusive GNAQ and GNA11 mutations were detected in 15 of 16 choroidal
naevi, at a low frequency { 28809862 }.
Histopathology:-
Choroidal and ciliary body naevi are composed of bland-looking cells that are generally plumper
than normal melanocytes. Four histological types of naevus cells can be identified: (1) plump
polyhedral cells, (2) slender spindle cells, (3) plump fusiform and dendritic cells, and (4) balloon
cells { 5924936 }. Naevus cells have oval or cigar-shaped nuclei that usually lack nucleoli and
nuclear membrane folding. The degree of cytoplasmic pigmentation is variable. Cytological atypia
and mitotic figures are absent.
Differential diagnosis
The main differential diagnoses are choroidal and ciliary body melanocytomas and melanomas of
spindle cell type, from which naevi can generally be differentiated on the basis of their smaller size
and the absence of cellular atypia and mitotic figures. Amelanotic naevi must additionally be
distinguished from other bland-looking spindle cell neoplasms, such as solitary fibrous tumour,
schwannoma, and neurofibroma. For ciliary body naevi, the differential diagnosis additionally
includes mesectodermal leiomyoma and adenoma or adenocarcinoma of the pigmented or
non-pigmented ciliary epithelium. Immunopositivity for markers such as melan-A and SOX10 has
proved helpful in confirming the melanocytic nature of uveal naevi.
Diagnostic molecular pathology:-
None
Staging (TNM):-
Not relevant.
Prognosis and prediction:-
Choroidal and ciliary body melanocytic naevi are benign lesions. They are monitored with clinical
observation and repeat diagnostic imaging with ultrasound and optical coherence tomography to
measure their thickness { 25091953 ; 23219068 ; 20346778 }. Approximately one third of choroidal
naevi show minor, slow growth during long-term follow-up { 20801518 }, but only a few undergo
malignant transformation { 16154197 }. It is estimated that the lifetime risk for malignant
transformation of a choroidal naevus is about 0.2% { 16650691 }.

Uveal melanocytoma
Definition:-
Uveal melanocytoma is an intensely pigmented subtype of ciliary body, iris, and choroidal naevi,
composed of large, plump, polyhedral cells.
ICD-O coding:-
Melanocytoma (of the ciliary body, iris, and choroid) 8726/0
Related terminology:-
Magnocellular naevus
Localization:-
At this anatomical site, melanocytoma arises in the stroma of the iris, with or without extension to
the chamber angle { 15767055 ; 20829742 }, ciliary body { 10768345 }, or choroid { 19125075 }.
See also Melanocytoma of the optic disc and optic nerve (p. 142).
Clinical features:-
Most iris melanocytomas are asymptomatic, and 70% are incidental findings { 15767055 }. About
15% of patients are first alerted by a dark spot on the iris; the rest by symptoms such as blurred
vision. At the time of diagnosis, about 50% of iris melanocytomas have seeded the adjacent iris
surface, chamber angle, or both, and some have formed satellites { 15767055 }. At baseline and by
15 years after diagnosis, respectively, approximately 5% and 50% of cases have caused
secondary glaucoma. Most iris melanocytomas are nodular with an irregular surface and lack
visible blood vessels, but one fifth are diffuse { 15767055 ; 21561663 }. The majority of ciliary body
melanocytomas probably go unrecognized; the diagnosed cases are typically those that erode
through the iris root, seed the anterior chamber and trabecular meshwork, focally shallow the
anterior chamber, induce a sectoral cataract, extend extraocularly, or have sentinel episcleral
vessels. The clinical appearance of choroidal melanocytomas may be similar to that of a choroidal
naevus or a small melanoma { 11786063 ; 17621182 }. In most cases, they form a discoid or
dome-shaped dark-grey or black mass that elevates the retina. In a few cases, the presence of
overlying orange pigment and subretinal fluid makes the distinction from choroidal melanoma even
more difficult { 19125075 }. Ultrasound biomicroscopy and anterior segment optical coherence
tomography are useful ancillary examinations for iris and ciliary body melanocytomas { 21377736 ;
23431723 ; 17621182 }. Fine-needle aspiration or cutter biopsy may aid in the diagnosis
{ 24371424 ; 15767055 ; 21794848 }.
Epidemiology:-
The incidence of uveal melanocytomas is unknown. They account for 3% of iris naevi { 15767055 }.
A total of < 100 ciliary body cases with pathological confirmation have been reported. Choroidal
melanocytomas are equally rare. The reported patient age at presentation ranges from infancy to
90 years { 9027679 ; 17621182 }. In children and adolescents, melanocytomas account for 7% of
melanocytic iris lesions { 22035581 }. The median age at diagnosis is 40–45 years. About 9% of iris
melanocytomas occur in Black patients, whereas only 2% of ordinary iris naevi occur in this
population { 15767055 ; 23290981 }. A female-to-male ratio of 2:1 was noted in the largest reported
series { 15767055 }. Aside from these findings, the distribution of melanocytomas by sex and in
different races is reported to be nearly equal { 19125075 }. In dark-skinned individuals, uveal
melanocytomas are more common than uveal melanomas { 10768345 ; 17621182 } [[Singh AD
(2007). Tumors of the uvea: benign melanocytic tumors. In: Singh AD, Damato BE, Pe’er J,
Murphree AL, Perry J, editors. Clinical ophthalmic oncology. Philadelphia: Elsevier; pp. 185–97.]].
Etiology:-
The etiology is unknown, but melanocytoma is assumed to be congenital.
Pathogenesis:-
Melanocytomas arise from melanocytes of neural crest origin.
Genetic profile
A genetic study of two ciliochoroidal melanocytomas revealed GNAQ mutations { 23685997 },
which were also present in 2 of 6 cases in another series: an iridociliary melanocytoma and an optic
nerve melanocytoma { 27239460 }. Melanocytomas with GNAQ mutations can rarely progress to
melanoma; those that do so have been found to have BAP1 mutations { 27239460 }.
Histopathology:-
On gross examination, the tumour appears dark brown to jet black, with full thickness. Most iris
melanocytomas measure 1–5 mm in diameter. Choroidal and ciliary body melanocytomas usually
replace the normal uvea and measure 1–5 mm in thickness. Ciliary body lesions may be larger than
other melanocytomas because they must attain a larger size before they can be detected. Their
abundant intracytoplasmic melanin pigment obscures cellular details. Foci of spontaneous necrosis
may be seen in larger melanocytomas. Paraffin-embedded sections must be bleached to reveal the
cytological detail and identify plump polyhedral cells with bland nuclei, small nucleoli, and a low N:C
ratio. Pigment dispersion and melanophages are frequent. Mitotic figures are absent or rare.
Differential diagnosis
The differential diagnosis includes iris, ciliary body, and choroidal melanomas; ordinary naevi in
these locations; adenoma and adenocarcinoma of the pigmented iris and ciliary epithelium or
retinal pigment epithelium; and cysts of the pigmented iris epithelium.
Prognosis and prediction:-
Iris and ciliary body melanocytomas may be monitored with clinical examination, photography,
ultrasound biomicroscopy, and optical coherence tomography. Those that involve, extend to, or
seed the chamber angle must be followed up for secondary melanocytomalytic glaucoma
{ 15767055 ; 28512420 ; 3658352 ; 17621182 }. Growth (sometimes rapid) and spontaneous
necrosis can occur { 28512420 ; 26692065 }. Resection may be warranted if the tumour grows or
induces secondary glaucoma. The prognosis for survival is excellent, except in rare instances of
malignant transformation to melanoma { 1078332 ; 19484449 ; 15965181 ; 8010703 ; 23685997 ;
17947283 }.

Bilateral diffuse uveal melanocytic hyperplasia
Definition:-
Bilateral diffuse uveal melanocytic hyperplasia is a paraneoplastic syndrome characterized by
diffuse proliferation of benign melanocytes in the uvea, in patients with an underlying extraocular
malignancy { 2322154 ; 14609707 }.
ICD-O coding:-
None
Related terminology:-
Bilateral diffuse uveal melanocytic proliferation (BDUMP); paraneoplastic uveal melanocytic
hyperplasia
Localization:-
Bilateral diffuse uveal melanocytic hyperplasia arises in the iris, ciliary body, and choroid.
Clinical features:-
Patients present with otherwise inexplicable rapid onset of bilateral vision loss refractory to
treatment. Unilateral ocular involvement is rare { 28005580 }. Important signs of bilateral diffuse
uveal melanocytic hyperplasia include multiple subtle red patches at the level of the retinal pigment
epithelium corresponding to areas of hyperfluorescence { 27861254 }, serous retinal detachment,
and rapidly progressive cataract { 2322154 ; 14609707 }. These ocular findings are associated with
(and often precede) the finding of underlying malignancy, most commonly carcinoma from the
ovary or endometrium in women and from the lung in men { 11176070 }. Less common primary
sites of the underlying primary neoplasm are the pancreas, lung, colon, and kidney { 27861254 ;
22803781 ; 14609707 }. Other paraneoplastic symptoms may coexist.
Epidemiology:-
The exact incidence of bilateral diffuse uveal melanocytic hyperplasia is unknown. The syndrome
seems to have an equal sex distribution and an average patient age at presentation of 63 years
(range: 34–89 years) { 11176070 }.
Etiology:-
In patients with bilateral diffuse uveal melanocytic hyperplasia, the IgG fraction contains a serum
factor called cultured melanocyte elongation and proliferation (CMEP) factor, which selectively
stimulates melanocyte proliferation { 22791177 }, although the exact mechanism is unknown.
Pathogenesis:-
Bilateral diffuse uveal melanocytic hyperplasia arises due to hyperplasia of uveal melanocytes; in
patients with this syndrome, melanocytes of the dermis, mucous membranes, and conjunctiva are
also affected { 19934829 ; 23788206 }.
Genetic profile
Recent molecular studies have shown gains in chromosomes 6 { 22803781 } and 5 { 24124896 },
as well as polysomy 8q { 27171825 }. Bilateral diffuse uveal melanocytic hyperplasia cells do not
show the chromosome 3 abnormalities or GNAQ mutations seen in uveal melanoma { 27171825 ;
22803781 }.
Histopathology:-
The uvea shows diffuse thickening secondary to proliferations of melanocytic cells in the choroid,
ciliary body, and iris. These proliferations consist of spindle-shaped cells with elongated oval nuclei,
inconspicuous nucleoli, and scant intracytoplasmic melanin pigment. Occasional cellular atypia has
been described, but mitoses are rare. The melanocytes stain positively for melan-A and HMB45
antigen, but do not overexpress p53 { 9355192 }, and they show a low proliferation rate { 27171825 ;
14609707 }. A serous detachment of the retina may overlie choroidal lesions.
Differential diagnosis
The differential diagnosis includes diffuse uveal melanoma.
Prognosis and prediction:-
The prognosis depends on the response of the primary tumour to therapy. Most patients die within
a year of the diagnosis, although there have been rare reports of longer survival { 14609707 }. Until
recently, the vision loss resulting from bilateral diffuse uveal melanocytic hyperplasia was
considered to be progressive and irreversible, but recent studies have shown stabilization of ocular
symptoms in patients treated with plasmapheresis { 25908835 }.

Choroidal and ciliary body melanomas
Definition:-
Choroidal and ciliary body melanomas are primary malignant melanocytic tumours.
ICD-O coding:-
Mixed epithelioid and spindle cell uveal melanoma 8770/3
Epithelioid cell uveal melanoma 8771/3
Spindle cell uveal melanoma NOS 8772/3
Spindle cell uveal melanoma, type A 8773/3
Spindle cell uveal melanoma, type B 8774/3
Related terminology:-
Not recommended: Uveal melanoma (less specific, because the choroid and ciliary body are
different parts of the uveal tract, which also includes the iris); intraocular melanoma (less specific,
because the iris is also intraocular tissue); ocular melanoma (inaccurate and discouraged, because
the term also includes extraocular conjunctival melanoma)
Localization:-
Melanoma can arise anywhere in the choroid and ciliary body, and it can extend from one to the
other. Choroidal and ciliary body melanomas can also extend to the iris, chamber angle, retina,
vitreous nerve, and optic nerve, as well as extraocularly { 17498805 ; 23116575 }.
Clinical features:-
Approximately 25% of patients with ciliary body or choroidal melanoma have no symptoms and are
diagnosed during routine eye examination { 25704938 }. Symptoms include photopsia (perceived
flashes of light), floaters, and loss of vision { 19606159 }. Occasionally, large choroidal and ciliary
body melanomas result in pain, redness of the eye, and glaucoma { 25704938 }. Multifocal uveal
melanomas have been reported { 10406619 }. Bilateral primary uveal melanoma occurs rarely
{ 12855969 ; 17952820 }.
Ciliary body melanomas are usually circumscribed and nodular. Diagnosis is often delayed
because the tumour is hidden behind the iris and is not apparent on routine examination. These
melanomas often encroach on and distort the crystalline lens, producing lens dislocation,
astigmatism, and unilateral cataract. The tumour can also produce focal shallowing of the anterior
chamber. Some ciliary body melanomas grow through the iris root and invade the anterior chamber,
simulating a primary iris melanoma (Fig #9732). Anterior chamber seeding and iris heterochromia
can develop in such cases, and secondary glaucoma is another complication. Glaucoma
mechanisms include anterior displacement of the lens-iris diaphragm, tumour seeding, and iris
neovascularization. Annular or ring melanomas usually involve more than 180° of the ciliary body
and show disproportionately less anteroposterior growth. The aqueous outflow pathways can be
obstructed by a ring melanoma { 10396198 ; 28159633 ; 25550867 }. Rare cases of melanomalytic
glaucoma develop when melanophages containing necrotic tumour debris physically obstruct the
trabecular meshwork { 26592671 }. In contrast, some eyes become hypotonous when the tumour
disrupts the ciliary epithelium, causing decreased production of aqueous humour. Hyphaema and
vitreous haemorrhage occur rarely when the tumour disrupts blood vessels or undergoes necrosis.
Ultrasonography, anterior segment optical coherence tomography, and transillumination are helpful
diagnostic techniques. External ocular signs that can suggest the diagnosis include dilated
episcleral blood vessels (so-called sentinel vessels) overlying the tumour and episcleral nodules
from extrascleral extension through emissary canals in the anterior sclera (Fig #9899).
Choroidal melanomas appear as a mass that is dome-shaped (in 75% of cases),
mushroom-shaped (in 20%), or diffuse (in 5%). They are pigmented in 55% of cases,
non-pigmented in 15%, and of mixed colour in 30% { 22466491 }. Their mean size is 11.3 mm in
basal diameter by 5.5 mm in thickness { 19667335 }. The mass is deep to the retina, without feeder
vessels, and there may be an overlying exudative retinal detachment, which is an important
presenting sign. Necrotic tumours cause painful uveitis, glaucoma, or both. Extraocular extension
may also occur, via vortex veins or emissary canals in the sclera and around the optic disc. The
tumour can also invade the optic nerve sheath, orbit, or both. Optic nerve invasion occurs in 5–6%
of cases { 16877391 ; 17223846 }.
The most common way to localize a choroidal melanoma is by direct visualization with direct and
indirect ophthalmoscopy. The tumour should be documented using fundus photography and
measured using A-scan and B-scan ultrasonography. Optical coherence tomography is generally
used to determine the effect of the tumour on the overlying and surrounding retina, including to
evaluate subretinal fluid. Fundus autofluorescence imaging can reveal orange lipofuscin pigment
overlying the tumour.
Epidemiology:-
The worldwide incidence of uveal melanoma is uncertain, but it is estimated to be about 7000
cases per year [[Kivelä T (2014). Incidence, prevalence and epidemiology of ocular melanoma. In:
Murray TG, Boldt HC, editors. Ocular melanoma: advances in diagnostic and therapeutic strategies.
London: Future Medicine; pp. 20–38.]], accounting for 5% of all primary melanomas { 3279559 }.
Choroidal and ciliary body melanomas are most common in White populations (in which 65% of all
cases are reported, although only 12% of the world’s population is White); they are rare in Asian
and African populations { 3279559 ; 18191091 ; 8459526 ; 21704381 }. Among Asians, the risk of
developing uveal melanoma is 1.2 times the risk among African-Americans; among Hispanic and
non-Hispanic White people, the risk is 5 times and 19 times (respectively) the risk among
African-Americans { 18387420 }. Worldwide, the incidence rate is about 2–8 cases per 1 million
person-years – lower at lower latitudes (1.3–2.9 cases per 1 million person-years) and increasing
with latitude (to 6.1–8.6 cases per 1 million person-years) { 17498805 ; 23116575 }. This incidence
has been relatively stable over a 25-year period { 15763193 ; 17498805 }. The mean patient age at
diagnosis, which has increased because of rising life expectancy, is currently 60–64 years in White
populations and 45 years among Asians { 15132378 ; 22557876 ; 7107133 ; 9645715 }. These
melanomas are rare in people aged < 20 years, accounting for only 0.4–0.8% of all cases
{ 26854035 ; 10900104 }. There is no sex predilection { 22079972 }. Choroidal and ciliary body
melanomas are more common among lightly pigmented than darkly pigmented individuals
{ 3279559 ; 16226513 ; 9627661 ; 12750097 }.
Etiology:-
Some choroidal and ciliary body melanomas develop de novo. Others develop from melanocytes in
uveal naevi (with a lifetime risk of 1 in 400) { 11864894 ; 16650691 ; 16154197 } or from
melanocytes in congenital ocular or oculodermal melanocytosis (naevus of Ota; with a lifetime risk
of 1 in 400) { 9442799 }. Risk factors include light skin pigmentation, blond or red hair,
light-coloured irides { 3857374 ; 2400347 }, dysplastic naevus syndrome { 15763193 }, and BAP1
tumour predisposition syndrome { 22935333 ; 9442799 ; 21874003 }. There is no definite role of
ultraviolet (UV) radiation exposure in the development of uveal melanoma { 8790610 ; 21704381 }.
Pathogenesis:-
Melanoma at this anatomical site is thought to arise from uveal melanocytes. Most cases develop
de novo, but uveal naevi can also transform into melanoma { 11864894 ; 16650691 ; 16154197 }.
Genetic profile
Two recurrent, mutually exclusive G protein driver mutations (in GNAQ and GNA11) have been
identified in uveal melanoma by mutation analysis { 27123562 ; 21083380 }. GNAQ and GNA11
encode heterotrimeric G protein alpha subunits of the Gq class { 16182515 } that are 90% identical
at the amino acid level and play redundant roles. Q209 and R183 mutations activate the MAPK
pathway and the Hippo tumour suppressor pathway { 24141786 ; 24882515 ; 24882516 }. Q209
mutations are more common than R183 mutations in uveal melanoma { 21083380 }. These
mutations appear to be an early event, and they are also present in blue naevi, choroidal naevi
{ 28809862 }, and naevi of Ota. BRAF mutations, which are a characteristic finding in cutaneous
melanoma, are very exceptional in uveal melanoma (Fig #9535).
Loss of chromosome 3 (monosomy 3), in particular when associated with polysomy 8q, is
associated with metastasis in choroidal melanoma { 19078957 }(Fig #9837). BAP1, a gene located
on chromosome 3 (at 3p21.1), has been found to carry inactivating mutations in about 80% of
metastasizing uveal melanomas { 21051595 }. BAP1 is a ubiquitin carboxy-terminal hydrolase.
Some mutations affect the catalytic domain of the enzyme; others are frameshift or nonsense
mutations resulting in a non-functional protein that is excluded from the nucleus. BAP1 appears to
be involved with chromatin remodelling { 20436459 } and cell-cycle control { 21484256 }.
BAP1-deficient cells exhibit stem cell–like qualities { 23915344 }. BAP1 also appears to be involved
in transendothelial migration and extravasation from the eye to the systemic circulation
{ 25506912 }. Once the neoplastic cells have entered the systemic circulation, a variety of factors,
including receptor/ligand gradients (e.g. between c-Met and its ligand HGF/SF) { 18281525 ;
18001467 ; 17261188 ; 9546344 } and the stoichiometric properties of the liver { 23392528 ;
27476775 }, are involved in the propensity of uveal melanoma to metastasize to the liver.
Mutations of codon 625 of SF3B1 on chromosome 2 are also found in some uveal melanomas
{ 23313955 }. SF3B1 is a splice factor involved in intron excision from pre-mRNA, and it may also
be associated with chromatin remodelling { 23313955 }. Another frequently mutated gene in uveal
melanoma is EIF1AX, located at Xp22.1 { 23793026 }; this gene encodes a translation initiation
factor. BAP1, SF3B1, and EIF1AX mutations appear to be mutually exclusive in uveal melanoma
{ 27123562 }.
Gene expression profiling has identified a set of differentially expressed genes in class 1A and 1B
(low and intermediate metastatic risk, respectively) versus class 2 (high metastatic risk) uveal
melanoma { 15492234 }. Further work has shown that class 1 melanomas retain a differentiated
melanocytic phenotype and transcriptome, whereas class 2 melanomas have a dedifferentiated,
cancer stem cell–like phenotype and transcriptome { 18477893 }. As the tumour progresses, it may
undergo SF3B1 or EIF1AX mutation (which appears to be associated with a class 1 gene
expression profile) or hemizygous BAP1 mutation (which, when associated with monosomy 3, is
associated with a class 2 gene expression profile) { 26933176 }. The preferentially expressed
antigen in melanoma (PRAME) has also been shown to be a biomarker for metastases in class 1
melanomas { 26933176 }.
In the liver, the tumour forms relatively dormant micrometastases that have the potential to grow
into clinical metastases { 23392528 ; 27476775 }. These metastases may form nodules (the
nodular pattern), infiltrate the hepatic parenchyma (the infiltrative pattern) { 27476775 }, or both. A
variety of factors may contribute to the growth of micrometastases into large metastases, including
likely abrogation of the host immune response (in particular NK cell function) { 10520219 ;
25683463 } and an increased local VEGF-to-PEDF ratio { 23793989 }.
A recent study by The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) categorized 80 uveal melanomas into four
subsets: two associated with poor-prognosis monosomy 3 and two with better-prognosis disomy 3
{ 28810145 }. The poor-prognosis monosomy 3 uveal melanomas were characterized by a distinct
global DNA methylation pattern and had distinct genomic, signalling, and immune profiles.
Genetic susceptibility
To date, the only known specific genetic susceptibility for choroidal and ciliary body melanomas is
BAP1 tumour predisposition syndrome, which is estimated to be present in approximately 2% of all
patients with uveal melanoma and 20% of those with familial cases { 25974357 ; 22545102 ;
27748099 ; 26096145 ; 26876698 }. In this syndrome, there is a germline mutation in BAP1. The
syndrome includes an increased risk of developing uveal melanoma, atypical Spitz tumours of the
skin, mesothelioma, cutaneous melanoma, clear cell renal cell carcinoma, and basal cell carcinoma
{ 27748099 ; 26096145 }. Other cancers suspected to be associated with the syndrome include
breast cancer, cholangiocarcinoma, non-small cell lung adenocarcinoma, meningioma, and
neuroendocrine carcinoma { 27748099 }. BAP1 tumour predisposition syndrome is inherited in an
autosomal dominant pattern. Diagnosis of the syndrome is based on the identification of a
heterozygous germline pathological variant in BAP1 by molecular genetic testing { 27748099 }.
White patients with ocular or oculodermal melanocytosis (naevus of Ota) have a 1 in 400 lifetime
risk of developing melanoma { 9442799 }.
Macroscopic appearance:-
Macroscopically, ciliary body melanomas are either dome-shaped or circumferential ring-shaped
tumours. Choroidal melanomas may exhibit a flat to domed shape when confined by the Bruch
membrane, or they may exhibit a characteristic so-called collar-button or mushroom-like shape
when they break through the Bruch membrane; the portion of the tumour that has broken through is
choked off by the membrane, resulting in vascular congestion (Figs #9733, #9834, #9835, #9836).
Histopathology:-
Microscopic examination should be used to assess for invasion of ocular structures (e.g. the
chamber angle, iris, sclera, retina, and optic nerve), as well as for extrascleral extension (Figs
#9734, #9908).
Variable amounts of melanin pigment are produced by these melanomas, which range from
amelanotic to mildly, moderately, or heavily pigmented. The cytological description of choroidal
melanoma was traditionally based on the Callender system, which in its modified form included four
types of tumours: spindle A (naevus-like) cell, spindle B cell, epithelioid cell, and mixed (spindle B
and epithelioid) cell { 6624832 }. Spindle A cells are fusiform and cohesive, and they have poorly
defined cell borders. Their spindle-shaped nucleus contains a central nuclear envelope fold (or
groove). Spindle B cells are fusiform and cohesive, and they contain spindle-shaped or cigar-like
nuclei and prominent nucleoli; this is the most frequent cell type in most choroidal melanomas.
Epithelioid cells are non-cohesive and contain large, round nuclei and abundant, prominent
eosinophilic cytoplasm; this cell type is associated with a high risk of metastasis. Intermediate cells
(small epithelioid cells) are intermediate between spindle B and epithelioid melanoma cells. Uveal
melanomas may undergo spontaneous necrosis. Tumours that exhibit 90–100% necrosis are
composed of so-called ghost melanoma cells; this phenomenon is probably due to a sequence of
events that starts with vascular insufficiency within the tumour, followed by cytokine release,
tumour necrosis, and secondary glaucoma { 27175363 }(Fig #9898). So-called balloon cells are
sometimes seen in uveal melanoma { 6882713 }. These cells have foamy, vacuolated cytoplasm
because of faulty production of melanin or degradation of melanosomes, and their cytoplasm
contains lipid. Balloon cells are often seen in choroidal melanoma after plaque brachytherapy or
proton beam irradiation { 1323252 }(Fig #9900). Both dendritic and scavenger tumour-infiltrating
macrophages are ubiquitous in uveal melanomas, and they can constitute a substantial portion of
the tumour mass { 11381040 }.
Although the cut-off points are arbitrary, spindle cell melanoma is defined as being composed of
spindle B cells in ≥ 90% of the tumour, and epithelioid cell melanoma is defined as being composed
of epithelioid cells in ≥ 90% of the tumour; all other tumours are mixed cell melanomas (the most
common type in many series) [[Kivelä T, Simpson ER, Grossniklaus HE, Jager MJ, Singh AD,
Caminal JM, et al. (2017)(Figs #9745, #9746, #9747). Uveal melanoma. In: Amin MB, Edge S,
Greene F, Byrd DR, Brookland RK, Washington MK, et al., editors. AJCC cancer staging manual.
8th ed. New York: Springer; pp. 805–17.]]. Epithelioid cell melanomas are associated with the worst
prognosis, and pure spindle cell melanomas are associated with the best prognosis { 15327098 }.
Because of the association between epithelioid cells and poor prognosis, some pathologists favour
simplifying the reporting to two categories: presence or absence of epithelioid cells in the tumour
[[Kivelä T, Simpson ER, Grossniklaus HE, Jager MJ, Singh AD, Caminal JM, et al. (2017). Uveal
melanoma. In: Amin MB, Edge S, Greene F, Byrd DR, Brookland RK, Washington MK, et al.,
editors. AJCC cancer staging manual. 8th ed. New York: Springer; pp. 805–17.]].
A number of special variants of choroidal and ciliary body melanomas may need to be considered.
Diffuse uveal melanoma is defined as being < 5 mm in thickness and occupying at least one
quarter of the uveal tract { 6966937 }. This growth pattern may be associated with a higher risk of
metastasis and extraocular growth, although the tumour is relatively thin. Retinoinvasive uveal
melanoma tends to evolve from a melanoma of the ciliary body, then invades the vitreous, the
retina, and through these tissues into the optic nerve { 29344483 ; 9349160 }. Other mechanisms of
optic nerve invasion by choroidal melanoma include via the retina after retinal invasion, directly
from the peripapillary choroid, and a combination of these mechanisms. A rare variant of ring
melanoma is anterior chamber angle melanoma, which leads to secondary glaucoma with minimal
involvement of the uveal tract { 11530045 ; 28159633 }. Another rare variant of uveal melanoma is
clear cell melanoma { 9230830 }; the tumour cells have oval, centrally located nuclei and clear
cytoplasm (due to the dissolution of glycogen granules during fixation). Clear cell melanomas must
be distinguished from renal cell carcinoma metastatic to the choroid. Cavitary melanomas of the
ciliary body contain extensive cavitary spaces { 9627662 }.
Choroidal melanomas are positive for HMB45 antigen, S100 protein, melan-A, MITF, tyrosinase,
and SOX10 { 17445277 ; 12742347 ; 11511840 ; 1762354 }. When using immunohistochemical
stains to identify choroidal melanoma, it is advisable to use at least two, such as HMB45 and S100
protein, in case one is negative. Additionally, MIB1 staining for Ki-67, a nuclear proliferation marker,
may be used to assess proliferative activity and has some prognostic relevance { 8664054 ;
7573354 }. Mitotic activity should be reported as the number of mitoses/mm2 { 8551926 }.
A number of other prognostic features of choroidal and ciliary body melanomas may also be
considered for reporting to the clinician. A lack of nuclear immunostaining for the BAP1 protein in
choroidal melanoma is strongly associated with an increased rate of metastasis { 25058347 ;
24633195 ; 27015033 }(Figs #9843, #9844). The following extravascular matrix patterns have been
identified, listed from least to most complex: normal, silent, straight, parallel, parallel with
cross-linking, arcs, arcs with branching, closed vascular loops, and vascular networks; the last
three patterns are associated with an increased risk of metastasis { 8371929 ; 10050870 }. It has
been proposed that the extravascular matrix patterns constitute vasculogenic mimicry { 10666364 ;
9135390 }. Large tumour cell nucleoli { 12766034 ; 9160022 } and a high microvascular density
also are associated with shorter survival { 12147581 ; 8674036 ; 10509639 }. Prominent
tumour-infiltrating lymphocytes and tumour-associated macrophages have been associated with an
increased likelihood of metastasis, and they are associated with high-risk chromosomal
abnormalities (in particular monosomy 3) { 20811059 ; 22743317 ; 28391358 ; 11381040 }.
Differential diagnosis
Choroidal and ciliary body melanomas must be differentiated from uveal naevus and
melanocytoma (magnocellular naevus), paraneoplastic bilateral diffuse uveal melanocytic
hyperplasia { 27171825 }, uveal metastasis, and primary spindle cell neoplasms. The differential
diagnosis of ciliary body melanomas also includes mesectodermal leiomyoma, iris and ciliary body
cysts, and adenoma and adenocarcinoma of the ciliary epithelium. Choroidal melanomas must be
distinguished from peripheral exudative haemorrhagic chorioretinopathy { 1915756 }, circumscribed
choroidal haemangioma, age-related macular degeneration with disciform scars and haemorrhage,
retinal pigment epithelial adenoma and adenocarcinoma, and congenital hypertrophy of the retinal
pigment epithelium { 16141866 }.
Staging (TNM):-
Uveal melanomas are staged according to the UICC TNM classification, 8th edition. There is a
separate staging for ciliary body and choroidal melanomas as distinct from iris melanomas.
According to the TNM classification system [[Brierly JD, Gospodarowicz MK, Wittekind C, editors
(2017). TNM classification of malignant tumours. 8th ed. Oxford: Wiley Blackwell.]], choroidal and
ciliary body melanomas are grouped by size as small (T1), medium (T2), large (T3), or very large
(T4), and they are staged (stage I–IV) according to size, ciliary body involvement, extraocular
extension, and presence of metastasis.
Prognosis and prediction:-
Tumour size, ciliary body involvement, and extraocular extension of the melanoma are of
prognostic significance. The risk of metastasis increases with increasing tumour size, and from
invasion of the ciliary body through extraocular invasion to invasion of both compartments
{ 25555246 } [[Kivelä T, Simpson ER, Grossniklaus HE, Jager MJ, Singh AD, Caminal JM, et al.
(2017). Uveal melanoma. In: Amin MB, Edge S, Greene F, Byrd DR, Brookland RK, Washington
MK, et al., editors. AJCC cancer staging manual. 8th ed. New York: Springer; pp. 805–17.]]
{ 23816968 }.
As many as 50% of patients in whom ciliary body or choroidal melanoma is diagnosed ultimately
die from haematogenous metastasis of the tumour, most commonly (in 90% of cases) to the liver
{ 11346394 ; 14578381 }. The prognosis for survival is worse for patients with melanomas of the
ciliary body and anterior choroid { 10922199 } and with larger tumours [[Kivelä T, Simpson ER,
Grossniklaus HE, Jager MJ, Singh AD, Caminal JM, et al. (2017). Uveal melanoma. In: Amin MB,
Edge S, Greene F, Byrd DR, Brookland RK, Washington MK, et al., editors. AJCC cancer staging
manual. 8th ed. New York: Springer; pp. 805–17.]]. Other common (often secondary) sites of
metastasis are lung (affected in 24% of cases) and bone (in 16%) { 11346394 }. The median
survival after detection of metastasis is 4–17 months, depending on how advanced the metastases
are when detected { 24403233 ; 27296487 }. In the past, when the liver was not imaged during
surveillance, the reported median survival rates for patients with metastases were 15–20% at 6
months and 10% at 1 year { 11346394 }; in a recent study in which most patients underwent regular
liver imaging, the reported rates were 75% at 6 months and 50% at 1 year { 27296487 }. Liver
function tests and imaging, including MRI, PET-CT, and ultrasound, have been used to detect liver
metastases { 14962411 }. Older age is a risk factor for decreased survival in patients with uveal
melanoma { 17365812 }. Patients with metastasis confined to extrahepatic sites have longer
survival times (of 19–28 months) than patients with hepatic metastases { 11346394 }.
The reported 5-year survival rates are 88% for the spindle cell type and 60% for the epithelioid or
mixed cell type overall { 11346394 }, but melanomas that have monosomy 3, are of gene
expression profile class 2, or show loss of nuclear BAP1 expression are associated with 5-year
survival rates of only about 30% { 25147369 }. Disomy 3 melanomas have a worse prognosis if
there is amplification of 8q { 9135991 }. Other negative prognostic factors include larger tumour size
{ 26596399 ; 28159971 ; 23816968 ; 27123792 }, extravascular matrix loops and networks
{ 25827538 ; 7777286 }, high microvascular density { 12147581 ; 8674036 ; 10509639 }, high
numbers of tumour-infiltrating macrophages { 20811059 ; 22743317 ; 28391358 ; 11381040 }, and
large tumour cell nucleoli { 12766034 ; 9160022 }.

Tumours metastatic to the uveal tract
Definition:-
Secondary tumours of the iris, ciliary body, and choroid are tumours that have metastasized to
these locations from a distant site.
ICD-O coding:-
Code according to the originating tumour type.
Clinical features:-
Most patients have known systemic cancer at the time of ocular diagnosis, but in as many as 34%
of patients, uveal metastases may precede the primary cancer diagnosis. In patients without a
known cancer, the primary site is identified as lung in 35% of cases and breast in 7% of cases, and
it remains unknown in about 50% of cases { 9261313 }.
The most common presenting symptoms are blurred vision and vision loss due to associated
exudative retinal detachment { 28399345 }. Anterior uveal metastases may abut and tilt the lens,
causing visual disturbance. Pain is a presenting feature in as many as 5–12% of cases { 12888048 ;
23988200 }, with floaters and photopsia occurring in 5–14% { 28399345 }; 9–13% of patients are
asymptomatic { 9261313 }.
Choroidal metastases are predominantly postequatorial, with as many as 40% located in the
macular region { 28399345 }. The mean number of metastases per eye is 1.4–1.6, with multiple and
bilateral tumours more commonly reported in breast cancer { 24169654 ; 9261313 }. Unilateral and
unifocal metastases are more commonly found with lung cancer { 23988200 }.
Secondary tumours in the choroid usually appear as cream-coloured or pale-yellow dome-shaped
or rounded masses. They may be associated with haemorrhage, necrosis, and/or subretinal fluid.
When the tumour appears orange in colour, an origin from renal carcinoma, thyroid carcinoma, or
neuroendocrine tumour should be considered { 28399345 }. Metastatic cutaneous melanoma may
be brownish-grey or amelanotic { 28399345 }.
Ciliary body metastases are usually amelanotic, and may be flat or dome-shaped. Secondary iris
tumours are usually white, but secondary renal or thyroid carcinoma may be pink or orange, and
melanoma may appear brown { 28399345 }.
Secondary iris tumours may present as stromal nodules or poorly defined thickening of the iris, or
they may be associated with feeder vessels, secondary glaucoma, corectopia, pseudohypopyon,
and hyphaema { 28076572 }.
Secondary choroidal tumours may show autofluorescence due to lipofuscin accumulation
{ 23429597 }; although this can be a useful identifying feature in some cases, it may also be seen in
choroidal melanoma.
Compared with choroidal melanoma, secondary tumours have a higher reflectivity on A-scan
ultrasonography and are acoustically solid on B-scan. The height-to-base ratio is typically much
lower than in melanoma { 25827542 }.
Optical coherence tomography shows that these metastases are usually undulating, associated
with thickening of the retinal pigment epithelium and subretinal fluid { 24743640 }.
Fluorescein angiography typically shows hypofluorescence in the early stages and
hyperfluorescence in the late stages (usually later than choroidal melanoma) { 23429597 }.
Indocyanine green angiography is also useful; it facilitates visualization of tumours through the
retina and can provide information about the surrounding vasculature.
CT is not usually useful, because of poor definition. On MRI, the choroidal mass usually appears
isointense on T1-weighted imaging and hypointense on T2-weighted imaging { 25827542 ;
28399345 }.
Biopsy may be useful, in particular if no primary site can be identified by systemic investigations
{ 25827542 ; 28399345 }.
Epidemiology:-
Uveal metastases are the most common intraocular malignancy, occurring in 8–10% of patients
with metastatic disease { 28399345 }. Because of the high-flow choroidal vasculature, metastases
are most common in the choroid (accounting for 88% of cases), with less-frequent involvement of
the iris (9% of cases) and ciliary body (2% of cases) { 9261313 }. The age and sex distribution
varies depending on the primary origin of the tumour.
Pathogenesis:-
Breast cancer is the most common secondary uveal tumour, accounting for 40–53% of choroidal
metastases { 12888048 }, with concurrent systemic metastases present in 92% of patients. Lung
cancer is the second most common secondary uveal tumour, accounting for 20–29% of cases
{ 23988200 }.
Less common primary sites for secondary carcinoma of the uveal tract are the gastrointestinal tract
(accounting for 4% of cases), prostate (2%), and kidney (2–4%). Rarer origins of metastasis
include the thyroid, testis, ovary, and bladder. Non-carcinoma primaries of secondary tumours
include cutaneous melanoma, neuroendocrine tumours, sarcomas, and lymphomas.
Genetic profile
The genetic profile can be useful in certain circumstances. It may be helpful in distinguishing
secondary cutaneous melanoma from primary uveal melanoma. Cutaneous melanoma commonly
shows BRAF mutations, which are not usually present in choroidal or ciliary body melanoma
(although they may occur in iris melanoma). Uveal melanomas may show chromosome 3 loss,
which does not occur in cutaneous melanoma. FISH for 1p36 and 8q is less helpful, because
amplification of these DNA regions can occur in cutaneous melanoma.
The genetic profile may also be useful for choosing therapeutic interventions in certain settings, for
example, ERBB2 (HER2) status in breast carcinoma, ALK and EGFR status in non-small cell lung
carcinoma, and KRAS status in colorectal adenocarcinoma; therefore, it is recommended where
possible to reserve some of the diagnostic sample (e.g. an intraocular biopsy) for molecular testing,
to aid in the further care of the patient.
Histopathology:-
Enucleation is relatively uncommon for secondary tumours of the uveal tract. Secondary tumours
can usually be distinguished from melanoma because they appear flatter and more undulating and
are often associated with serous retinal detachment and exudation. Occasionally, tumours may
spread through the Bruch membrane and develop a mushroom or collar-button shape, similar to
that of uveal melanoma. However, secondary tumours are usually yellow or cream-coloured, in
contrast to the majority of melanomas, which are usually at least in part pigmented.
The pathology of secondary tumours depends on the primary site of origin. Metastatic breast
carcinoma is most commonly of the invasive ductal type, but lobular and other subtypes have also
been described { 12888048 }. For metastatic lung carcinoma, both small cell carcinoma and
non-small cell carcinoma have been described, but metastases from non-small cell carcinoma
predominate, accounting for 80% of cases (with metastases from small cell carcinoma accounting
for the other 20%); this corresponds with the primary cancer incidence { 23988200 }. The most
common type of non-small cell lung carcinoma is adenocarcinoma, followed by squamous cell
carcinoma. Metastatic colorectal adenocarcinoma has a characteristic histology, often showing
necrotic debris (so-called dirty necrosis) in glands, whereas tumours of the upper gastrointestinal
and pancreaticobiliary tracts may be morphologically indistinguishable from lung or breast
carcinoma. Renal carcinoma may have classic clear cell morphology or may be more poorly
differentiated, even sarcomatoid. Thyroid carcinoma may show colloid production. Neuroendocrine
tumours typically have a packeted architecture, with a rich vascular supply. Cutaneous melanoma
may be indistinguishable from uveal melanoma on morphology.
Morphological features may give clues as to the primary origin, but immunohistochemical staining
is the most useful ancillary technique for ascertaining the primary site of origin. The most commonly
used immunohistochemical markers are shown in Table #10224.
Prognosis and prediction:-
Uveal metastases tend to become apparent late in the course of malignancy, and they are
associated with disseminated disease and poor prognosis. With choroidal metastasis, the median
survival time is 6 months (range: 0.5–47 months) { 24169654 }. Patients with metastatic lung
cancer have a shorter survival time than do patients with breast cancer, and survival time is shorter
when there are also extraocular sites of metastasis.
The most common treatment modality is external beam radiation therapy, which results in tumour
regression in about 94% of cases, with conservation of vision in about 75% { 25827542 ;
28399345 }; bilateral treatment should be considered, because metastasis may later occur in the
other eye. Other local treatments include brachytherapy, photodynamic therapy, VEGF inhibitors,
and surgical resection { 25827542 ; 28399345 }. Systemic treatments may be administered in the
case of relapsed disseminated disease.
